The National Lung Screening Trial (NLST) compared the efficacy of low-dose helical CT with that of standard chest X-ray for detecting lung cancer. Low-dose CT resulted in 20% reduction in lung cancer mortality, and the US Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) recommends CT lung screening for those 50-80 years of age, minimum 30 pack-years of smoking, and no more than 15 years since quitting. The Risk-based NLST Outcomes Tool (RNOT) provides chance of diagnosis and death from lung cancer, and risk of false-positive CT screen, based on data from the NLST.

The best way to lower your risk of lung cancer, and all smoking-related diseases, is to quit smoking. Learn more by visiting smokefree.gov and the CDC page on quitting smoking.

Risk Tool

The task force does not recommend that you get screened if you are not between the ages of 50 and 80, or if you are a non smoker, or if you are a current smoker with less than 30 pack-year of smoking or if you are a former smoker who quitted for more than 15 years.
