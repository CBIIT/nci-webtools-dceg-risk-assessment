USPSTF (U.S. Preventive Services Task Force) compares the efficacy of low-dose helical CT with that of standard chest X-ray for detecting lung cancer. The initial findings reveal that low-dose CT resulted in 20% reduction in lung cancer mortality among participants satisfying the USPSTF inclusion criteria (55-80 years of age, minimum 30 pack-years of smoking and no more than 15 years since quitting). But it is yet unknown if the risk of lung cancer death changes the benefits/harms of low-dose CT screening. The Risk-based NLST Outcomes Tool (RNOT) is a statistical analysis tool that predicts a person's risk of lung cancer death based on a series of demographic and clinical risk factors for lung cancer. Findings from the tool support defining the high-risk targeting population for low-dose CT screening where benefits of screening overweigh harms thus improving the selection process for lung-cancer screening.

The best way to lower your risk of lung cancer, and all smoking-related diseases, is to quit smoking. Learn more by visiting smokefree.gov and the CDC page on quitting smoking.

Risk Tool

The task force does not recommend that you get screened if you are not between the ages of 55 and 80, or if you are a non smoker, or if you are a current smoker with less than 30 pack-year of smoking or if you are a former smoker who quitted for more than 15 years.
